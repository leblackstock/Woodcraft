## The One-Line Version

A small cedar shop in Lovejoy, Georgia. Plainspoken, specific, southern, unfussy. The work speaks; the copy gets out of the way.

## One Voice, Four Modes

Drakkar Designs uses one shared voice across all customer-facing writing. Catalogs, brand posts, Marketplace listings, and customer replies do not have separate or competing voices. They use the same core rules in this guide with a mode that adjusts emphasis for the use case.

Every mode must preserve:

- plainspoken, specific, local, and unfussy language
- approved facts and truthful claims
- no first person
- the shared vocabulary and banned-word rules
- short, confident sentence shape
- restraint instead of hype

### Catalog Mode

Use for retail catalogs, product books, catalog cards, and catalog-style product blurbs.

- Keep it quiet, concise, grounded, and slightly poetic.
- Let specificity and restraint carry the tone.
- Prefer short editorial phrases and calm product descriptions.
- Avoid loud promotional language and hard-sell calls to action.

### Brand Post Mode

Use for Facebook Page posts, Instagram captions, maker/process posts, and other trust-building brand content.

- Allow more feeling, personality, and expressive rhythm than catalog copy.
- Use short maker, shop, cedar, place, or process details to create connection.
- Keep claims grounded and specific.
- Do not turn emotional language into hype, fake luxury, or generic inspiration copy.

### Marketplace Mode

Use for Facebook Marketplace titles, descriptions, factual graphic text, and listing-focused calls to action.

- Be direct, practical, factual, and easy to scan.
- Lead with the product and the buyer-relevant details.
- Make price, size, material, ordering, pickup/delivery, and lead-time information easy to find when approved.
- Use simple factual calls to action. Do not turn the listing into a dramatic brand story.

### Customer Reply Mode

Use for buyer messages, inquiry replies, quote follow-ups, pickup/delivery coordination, and customer-service responses.

- Answer the customer’s question first.
- Be helpful, conversational, concise, and logistics-focused.
- State what is known, ask clearly for what is needed, and avoid overexplaining.
- Do not invent availability, timing, prices, policies, or commitments to sound helpful.

### Mode Selection Rule

- Name the applicable mode in Claude handoffs and reusable writing prompts.
- If a task spans multiple outputs, assign a mode to each output instead of blending the modes.
- If no mode is named, infer it from the requested asset type. Ask only when the asset type is genuinely ambiguous.
- Modes adjust emphasis only. They never override the core voice, approved-fact requirements, or banned-word rules.

## Claude Approval Boundary

Claude approval is required for final publishable customer-facing prose blocks, including Marketplace listing titles and descriptions, Facebook or Instagram brand-post captions, advertisement headlines and body copy, catalog prose and product blurbs, customer reply templates, and similar standalone text meant to be published as copy.

Claude approval is not required for prompt-writing or prep artifacts themselves. GPT/Codex may prepare prompts, prompt packs, image prompts, internal notes, outline language, structured fact packages, and Claude handoff prompts without Claude approval. Those artifacts may include proposed image words, labels, price text, catalog marks, and other literal in-image text. The Claude gate applies only when the output is the actual publishable text block for a listing, post or caption, advertisement, catalog, or customer reply.

Image graphic text and literal words embedded in image prompts are governed by the image workflow, not by the Claude final-copy gate. When the active image workflow assigns that work to GPT/Codex, those words may be generated without Claude approval, but they must still use approved facts, the applicable voice mode, and the documented image-text rules.

## Who We Are

- A one-person workshop. He builds, his wife handles the rest.
- Lovejoy, Georgia. Don't drop the location — earn it.
- Self-taught, sharp on the details, at the bench since boyhood.
- Right now we sell cedar garden goods. Eventually he'll build fancier things. The voice is meant to grow with him.

## Who We Are Not

- Not a "studio." Not a "brand." Not "artisans."
- Not vikings. The DD monogram does that work; the words don't.
- Not corporate. No "thoughtfully crafted," no "curated," no "elevated," no "experiences."
- Not folksy-fake. Don't reach for "y'all" or backwoods cosplay.

---

## Pronouns And People

- **No first person.** No "I," "we," "my," "us," "our."
- Refer to him obliquely: "the shop," "the bench," "one pair of hands," "the same pair of hands," "his keen eye."
- Do not name him. His name is Scandinavian; the clientele won't get it.
- Do not name his wife either. Keep the brand anonymous and let the work be the face.

## Words We Use

These belong in the vocabulary:

- **cedar** (always — never "wood" when "cedar" is true)
- **bench, shop, joinery, finish, cut, fit, sand, mark**
- **porch, patio, doorstep, stoop, rail, walkway, driveway**
- **boxwood, hydrangea, magnolia, herbs, basil, thyme, lavender, succulents** — specific plants are more vivid than "flowers"
- **handmade, built, sized, fits**
- **western red cedar** (the wood spec — say it in full when relevant)
- **rot-resistant, aromatic, slow to weather** (cedar's actual virtues)
- **made to order, by quote, by request, on first conversation**

## Words To Avoid

These are banned or strongly suspect:

| Banned | Why |
| --- | --- |
| artisan, artisanal | AI slop / craft-fair cliché |
| curated | corporate; nothing here is curated |
| thoughtfully, lovingly, carefully crafted | empty filler |
| handcrafted | "handmade" is enough |
| raised by hand, hewn, forged | vikings; we don't go there |
| elevate, experience, journey, story | startup-speak |
| sustainable, eco-friendly, environmentally friendly | claim it by showing it (cedar, made-to-order, no overproduction) — don't say it |
| premium (except material grade, e.g. premium grade pine), luxury, bespoke | avoid "premium" as a general quality claim; allow it only when naming a wood/material grade. Visual or typography guidance may use "premium" to describe appearance, but that does not approve the word for customer-facing copy |
| timeless, heirloom | overused |
| crafted | just say "made" or "built" |
| our (anything) | first person rule |
| Net 30, MOQ, terms (in customer copy) | partner-confidential; the partner conversation handles it |

## Reading Level

Aim around 6th–8th grade. The clientele isn't reading for vocabulary. If a word feels like it's earning style points instead of meaning, swap it for the plainer one.

- "particular" → "sharp on the details" or "with a keen eye"
- "meticulous" → "careful" / "won't rush a piece"
- "purveyor" → just don't

## Sentence Shape

- **Short sentences. Real punctuation.** Periods over commas. Periods over em dashes too — a dash almost always wants to be a period or a colon instead. The dash is reserved for short pull lines and section openers, not product blurbs or body copy.
- **Lists of three** are the workhorse rhythm — "Cut, fit, and finished." "Tall, classic, and small." "Cedar. Stainless. Tung oil."
- **Sensory or specific first, abstract second** — if you can lead with a number, a plant, a place, or a verb, do.

### Good

> One man, one bench, one piece at a time.

> Nothing leaves the shop until the joinery sits flat and the work meets his keen eye.

> Just shy of four feet long. Runs along a porch rail or under a wide window — a hedge of herbs, a line of boxwood.

### Less Good

> Our team of dedicated craftsmen creates beautifully handcrafted heirloom-quality planters using only the finest sustainable materials.

(Reasons: "team," "dedicated," "beautifully handcrafted," "heirloom-quality," "finest," "sustainable" — all of it.)

## Catalog Wording Style

Catalog wording should be plainspoken, grounded, and slightly poetic. Prefer short sentences. Avoid hype, exaggerated claims, and overexplaining.

Good catalog phrase references include:

- "One man, one bench, one project at a time."
- "Built to order, one at a time."
- "Most pieces can be sized to suit."
- "The everyday squares."
- "Stacked and set."
- "Up off the ground."
- "The biggest and the smallest."

Use these as tone references, not as required or automatically inserted text. Final publishable customer-facing catalog prose must still come from Claude and use approved facts. Prompt drafts, image prompt wording, and governed image graphic text follow the Claude Approval Boundary above.

## Product Blurbs — Formula

Every blurb has three jobs:

1. **One specific construction or shape detail** ("slatted seat, sloped back"; "wider at the rim than at the foot")
2. **One specific use or scene** ("by the front door"; "against a porch rail"; "where the curb meets the driveway")
3. **One named plant or scale anchor**, when it fits ("a young Japanese maple"; "the size of a generous bookend")

Horizontal cards (3 per page): keep it to about 2 short sentences.
Vertical cards (2 per page): allow 2–3 sentences with a small finishing line.

Never repeat words across the three jobs. Never explain a piece by listing every possible plant or every possible use; pick one or two that feel true to the shape.

## What "Fancy" Means Here

Right now we're selling cedar planters that come from Home Depot lumber. We're not pretending otherwise. The "fancy" lives in:

- **Restraint** — what we don't say
- **Specificity** — the right plant, the right dimension, the right doorstep
- **Confidence** — declarative sentences, not hedges
- **Typography and layout** — let the design do the elevating

If a sentence feels like it's straining to sound fancy, it isn't. Cut it back and start over.

## Edge Cases

- **He sometimes leaves pieces rustic instead of fully sanded.** Don't promise "sanded smooth" as a universal. Say "the work meets his keen eye" — covers both.
- **The wood ships in through Home Depot.** Don't claim local milling, forest sourcing, mileage, or any specific origin chain. "Western red cedar" is the truthful spec.
- **Terms are not strong yet.** Don't lock in Net 30 / MOQ / deposit numbers in customer-facing copy. Use phrases like "partner terms set on first conversation."
- **Location wording is workflow-specific.** Keep the voice local, but use the current workflow-approved location wording. For current FBM work, prefer "locally in Georgia"; use the exact city only when logistics require it.
- **SKU facts do not live here.** Included items, exclusions, sizes, customization availability, and other product-specific facts must come from current product, listing, or workflow records.

## When In Doubt

Read it out loud. If it sounds like a person who builds things, keep it. If it sounds like a brand consultant who's never touched cedar, rewrite.
