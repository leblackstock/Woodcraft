## Shared Typography Identity

Drakkar Designs uses one shared typography identity across all graphics, with the intensity adjusted for the use case.

- Catalog text is refined, editorial, quiet, structured, and premium.
- Facebook brand-post text is expressive, dramatic, artistic, rustic, and poster-like.
- Facebook Marketplace listing text is cleaner, simpler, clearer, and more sales-functional.
- Every format should retain the same premium rustic catalog identity, palette, hierarchy, and handcrafted cedar feel.

Rule of thumb:

- Catalog text should feel like a refined product book.
- Brand-post text should make people feel something.
- Marketplace listing text should help people understand and message quickly.

## Core Lettering Feel

Text should feel handcrafted, heritage-minded, catalog-inspired, slightly Norse/rustic, refined, and premium.

It should not feel like:

- a plain ad template
- a cheap sales flyer
- a generic corporate brand
- a cheesy Viking theme
- flat, uniform, template-made typography

## Catalog Type System Reference

The saved Drakkar retail catalog is the strict reference for catalog-style typography. Its source CSS establishes this type system:

| Role | Catalog type direction | Use |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Display and editorial headlines | Cormorant Garamond, Cormorant, or a similarly elegant old-style serif | Covers, section titles, short display headlines, and product names |
| Body and supporting copy | Manrope or a similarly clean, restrained sans serif | Paragraphs, supporting lines, and readable explanations |
| Labels and technical details | JetBrains Mono, IBM Plex Mono, or a similarly refined monospaced face | Small uppercase labels, dimensions, metadata, section markers, and price labels |

Use the named catalog typefaces for catalog work when available. In generated graphics, preserve their visual roles and overall feel rather than forcing an unreliable exact-font imitation.

## Brand Palette For Text

| Role | Color | Hex |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Primary dark text or background | Deep charcoal | `#13181B` |
| Primary light text or background | Warm stone/cream | `#DEDCD6` |
| Softer light background | Soft cream | `#ebe9e3` |
| Emphasis and accent | Cedar/burnt orange | `#CF4E17` |
| Quiet metadata and dividers | Muted silver | `#A4A9A5` |
| Context color for contrast decisions | Warm natural cedar wood tones | Maintain clear text contrast; do not use as a primary text color without testing readability |

Color rules:

- Use cream/stone as the primary readable text color on dark backgrounds.
- Use deep charcoal as the primary readable text color on light backgrounds.
- Use cedar/burnt orange as an accent, not as the main color for every line.
- Use muted silver for subtle dividers, secondary text, captions, or quiet detail labels.
- Orange emphasis works best on key words, short labels, rules, button-like treatments, icons, or small ornaments.

## General Text Style Rules

- Use creative typography hierarchy while staying readable.
- Mix large headline words, medium supporting lines, and small detail text.
- Use cream/stone for most lettering.
- Use cedar/burnt-orange highlights for key emphasis words such as `CEDAR`, `HANDMADE`, `BUILT TO LAST`, `GEORGIA`, `LOCAL`, or `CUSTOM`.
- Allow major headline words to have slightly distressed, stamped, carved, weathered, letterpress, or rustic texture when appropriate.
- Keep supporting text cleaner and easier to read.
- Use elegant spacing, thin divider lines, small ornaments, icon-style labels, badges, and accent rules when appropriate.
- Do not make every word the same size, color, font, or weight.
- Allow artistic variation as long as the typography stays on-brand.
- Use spacing and contrast to create rhythm instead of cramming text together.
- Keep social and Marketplace text readable on mobile.
- Do not add random extra words that were not requested.
- Avoid generic modern corporate typography.
- Avoid cheesy Viking fonts and unreadable fantasy lettering.

## Shared Hierarchy Guidance

- Headlines carry the mood and primary visual impact.
- Important words may be larger, bolder, textured, or cedar-orange.
- Supporting lines should be smaller, cleaner, and easier to read.
- Location or maker details can be treated as small premium label text.
- Short calls to action can be set apart with a badge, rule, button-like treatment, or accent line.
- Use scale, spacing, weight, color, and alignment together to create a clear reading order.

## Readability Rules

- Prioritize legibility over decoration, texture, or exact typeface matching.
- Keep small supporting text clean, high-contrast, and free of distressing.
- Give headlines enough line spacing and breathing room to remain readable.
- Use tracking deliberately: wider for small uppercase labels, restrained for body text, and tighter only when a large display headline benefits from it.
- Avoid long paragraphs in social and Marketplace graphics.
- Check social and Marketplace typography at mobile viewing size before approval.

## Catalog Text Style

Catalog typography should feel:

- editorial
- refined
- quietly premium
- structured
- spacious
- handmade but not messy
- slightly old-world or heritage-minded
- catalog-like rather than ad-like

Catalog characteristics:

- Use generous spacing and breathing room.
- Use thin divider lines and small accent rules.
- Use small uppercase tracking for labels, section headings, page headers, SKU/category markers, and metadata.
- Use elegant serif-style headlines for main section titles.
- Use restrained body copy with clean, readable paragraph text.
- Use cedar/burnt orange sparingly for section numbers, small labels, price boxes, emphasis words, and accent lines.
- Use muted silver/gray for quiet metadata, dividers, captions, and secondary labels.
- Pair catalog typography with warm stone/cream or deep charcoal according to the page mood while preserving clear text contrast.
- Use product names as calm editorial headings, not loud ad headlines.
- Keep price areas structured with thin rectangular rules or clean label boxes.
- Use small label text for photo captions, SKU tags, category markers, and page references.
- Avoid oversized promotional typography except on a cover or divider page.
- Avoid loud flyer-style badges, heavy buttons, crowded text, and excessive distressed texture.
- Make the result feel like a boutique maker’s product book, not a social media ad.

### Catalog Hierarchy

| Role | Direction |
| --- | --- |
| Page or brand header | Small uppercase, widely tracked, and quiet |
| Section label | Small uppercase with a section number and cedar-orange accent |
| Main headline | Large, elegant, serif-like, calm, and given generous breathing room |
| Subhead | Smaller, italic, or refined supporting line where useful |
| Product name | Medium-large, readable, and refined |
| Dimensions | Smaller monospaced or technical-feeling text |
| Body copy | Clean, readable, and modest in size |
| Notes | Small label, often with cedar-orange `NOTE` emphasis |
| Price | Structured label box, never flashy |
| Footer or page marker | Tiny, tracked, and quiet |

For catalog wording, tone, vocabulary, sentence shape, and approved phrase references, follow `00_brand/VOICE.md`.

## Facebook Brand Post Text Style

- Make typography more expressive, dramatic, and poster-like than catalog text.
- Allow bigger font-size contrast.
- Use rustic or distressed headline texture more heavily when it adds character.
- Use decorative dividers, badges, ornaments, catalog marks, and icon-style feature rows where useful.
- Split headline words across multiple lines for visual impact when readability remains strong.
- Keep the result premium, handcrafted, local, memorable, and connected to the catalog identity.
- Do not let it become a plain sales flyer.

## Facebook Marketplace Listing Text Style

- Keep typography cleaner, simpler, and more practical than brand-post typography.
- Keep it on-brand, but make readability and clarity the first priority.
- Make the product name the largest text.
- Give price, size, and order details a clear secondary hierarchy.
- Use cedar/burnt orange for price emphasis, key dividers, small badges, or call-to-action accents.
- Use less ornament than a brand post.
- Use distressed or rustic texture lightly, mostly on short product names or major headings.
- Avoid tiny decorative type and long paragraphs.
- Make the result trustworthy, clear, handmade, and easy to act on.
- Do not make it look like a dramatic brand poster unless specifically requested.

## Rustic Texture Guidance

- Use distressed, stamped, carved, weathered, or letterpress texture only where it adds character.
- Apply texture mainly to major headline words or brand-emphasis words.
- Brand posts may use more rustic texture.
- Catalog text should use little to no distressed texture except on logo/brand marks or special cover and divider moments.
- Marketplace listing text should use texture sparingly.
- Never distress small text when it reduces readability.
- Keep the final look handcrafted and premium, not dirty, cheap, or grungy.

## Ornament And Divider Guidance

- Thin divider lines, small Norse/catalog-inspired ornaments, corner marks, accent rules, badges, and icon-style labels are allowed.
- Ornament should support the text layout, not overpower it.
- Keep ornament subtle, refined, and catalog-like.
- Catalog layouts use the most restrained ornament.
- Brand posts may use more ornament.
- Marketplace listing text uses less ornament and stays clearer.
- Avoid cartoon Viking motifs, horned helmets, shields, fantasy runes, and clipart.

## Reusable Typography Prompt Blocks

### General

> “Use refined catalog-inspired typography with creative hierarchy: varied font sizes, cream and cedar-orange emphasis, rustic/distressed headline texture where appropriate, thin divider lines, small Norse/catalog ornaments, and readable mobile-friendly layout. Do not make the text flat or uniform; allow artistic variation while keeping it premium and on-brand.”

### Catalog

> “Typography direction: follow the Drakkar Designs catalog style with refined editorial hierarchy, generous spacing, elegant serif-like headlines, small uppercase tracked labels, restrained body copy, thin divider lines, cedar-orange accent rules, muted silver metadata, and structured price or note labels. Keep it calm, premium, readable, handmade, and catalog-like rather than promotional.”

### Facebook Brand Post

> “Typography direction: use expressive premium rustic poster-style lettering with strong hierarchy, large dramatic headline words, cream/stone text, cedar-orange emphasis, occasional distressed or weathered headline texture, refined spacing, thin divider lines, small heritage-inspired ornaments, and a handcrafted catalog feel. Keep it readable, artistic, premium, and on-brand.”

### Facebook Marketplace

> “Typography direction: use clean, practical, sales-focused lettering that still matches the Drakkar Designs premium rustic catalog style. Make the product name largest, price/details medium, and notes smaller. Use cream/stone and charcoal text with cedar-orange highlights, simple divider lines, restrained rustic styling, and mobile-friendly readability. Keep it clear, trustworthy, and easy to act on.”

### Short Version For Image Prompts

> “Typography direction: premium rustic catalog-style lettering with varied hierarchy, cream/stone text, cedar-orange emphasis, occasional distressed headline texture, refined spacing, subtle divider lines, and small heritage-inspired ornaments. Keep it readable, polished, handcrafted, and on-brand.”
