# Drakkar Designs — Voice Guide

A working reference for anyone (or anything) writing copy for Drakkar
Designs. Read this before you write a single word.

---

## The one-line version

A small cedar shop in Lovejoy, Georgia. Plainspoken, specific, southern,
unfussy. The work speaks; the copy gets out of the way.

---

## Who we are

- A one-person workshop. He builds, his wife handles the rest.
- Lovejoy, Georgia. Don't drop the location — earn it.
- Self-taught, sharp on the details, at the bench since boyhood.
- Right now we sell cedar garden goods. Eventually he'll build fancier
  things. The voice is meant to grow with him.

## Who we are NOT

- Not a "studio." Not a "brand." Not "artisans."
- Not vikings. The DD monogram does that work; the words don't.
- Not corporate. No "thoughtfully crafted," no "curated," no
  "elevated," no "experiences."
- Not folksy-fake. Don't reach for "ya'll" or backwoods cosplay.

---

## Pronouns and people

- **No first person.** No "I," "we," "my," "us," "our."
- Refer to him obliquely: "the shop," "the bench," "one pair of hands,"
  "the same pair of hands," "his keen eye."
- Do not name him. His name is Scandinavian; the clientele won't get it.
- Do not name his wife either. Keep the brand anonymous and let the
  work be the face.

---

## Words we use

These belong in the vocabulary:

- **cedar** (always — never "wood" when "cedar" is true)
- **bench, shop, joinery, finish, cut, fit, sand, mark**
- **porch, patio, doorstep, stoop, rail, walkway, driveway**
- **boxwood, hydrangea, magnolia, herbs, basil, thyme, lavender,
  succulents** — specific plants are more vivid than "flowers"
- **handmade, built, sized, fits**
- **western red cedar** (the wood spec — say it in full when relevant)
- **rot-resistant, aromatic, slow to weather** (cedar's actual virtues)
- **made to order, by quote, by request, on first conversation**

## Sentence shape

- **Short sentences. Real punctuation.** Periods over commas.
  Periods over em-dashes too — a dash almost always wants to be a
  period or a colon instead. The dash is reserved for short pull
  lines and section openers, not product blurbs or body copy.
- **Lists of three** are the workhorse rhythm — "Cut, fit, and
  finished." "Tall, classic, and small." "Cedar. Stainless. Tung oil."
- **Sensory or specific first, abstract second** — if you can lead with
  a number, a plant, a place, or a verb, do.

### Good

> One man, one bench, one piece at a time.

> Nothing leaves the shop until the joinery sits flat and the work
> meets his keen eye.

> Just shy of four feet long. Runs along a porch rail or under a wide
> window — a hedge of herbs, a line of boxwood.

### Less good

> Our team of dedicated craftsmen creates beautifully handcrafted
> heirloom-quality planters using only the finest sustainable
> materials.

(Reasons: "team," "dedicated," "beautifully handcrafted,"
"heirloom-quality," "finest," "sustainable" — all of it.)

## Product blurbs — formula

Every blurb has three jobs:

1. **One specific construction or shape detail** ("slatted seat,
   sloped back"; "wider at the rim than at the foot")
2. **One specific use or scene** ("by the front door"; "against a
   porch rail"; "where the curb meets the driveway")
3. **One named plant or scale anchor**, when it fits ("a young
   Japanese maple"; "the size of a generous bookend")

Horizontal cards (3 per page): keep it to ~2 short sentences.
Vertical cards (2 per page): allow 2â€“3 sentences with a small
finishing line.

Never repeat words across the three jobs. Never explain a piece by
listing every possible plant or every possible use; pick one or two
that feel true to the shape.

## What "fancy" means here

Right now we're selling cedar planters that come from Home Depot
lumber. We're not pretending otherwise. The "fancy" lives in:

- **Restraint** — what we don't say
- **Specificity** — the right plant, the right dimension, the right
  doorstep
- **Confidence** — declarative sentences, not hedges
- **Typography and layout** — let the design do the elevating

If a sentence feels like it's straining to sound fancy, it isn't.
Cut it back and start over.

## Edge cases

- **He sometimes leaves pieces rustic instead of fully sanded.**
  Don't promise "sanded smooth" as a universal. Say "the work meets
  his keen eye" — covers both.
- **The wood ships in through Home Depot.** Don't claim local milling,
  forest sourcing, mileage, or any specific origin chain. "Western
  red cedar" is the truthful spec.
- **Terms are not strong yet.** Don't lock in Net 30 / MOQ / deposit
  numbers in customer-facing copy. Use phrases like "partner terms
  set on first conversation."
- **The Q (Mini Cube) image shows flowers — they're not included.**
  Disclose plainly: "flowers not included."
- **Custom sizing exists on D, J, and K.** Default phrasing:
  "custom sizes by quote" or "height made to fit your driveway."

---

## When in doubt

Read it out loud. If it sounds like a person who builds things, keep
it. If it sounds like a brand consultant who's never touched cedar,
rewrite.
