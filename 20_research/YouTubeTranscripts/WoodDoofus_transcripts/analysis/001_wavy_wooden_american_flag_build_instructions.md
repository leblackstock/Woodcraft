## Build Steps

1. Cut the boards into blanks.
   - Start with three 2x12 x 10 ft boards.
   - Cut them into 24 in lengths.
   - Each flag is reported as 24 in wide, so the 24 in length becomes the flag width.

2. Rip the strips.
   - Rip the 24 in blanks into 1 in strips on the table saw.
   - Each flag uses 13 strips for 13 stripes.
   - A batch of ten flags requires 130 strips.
   - Treat this as a high-focus, high-repetition table-saw operation. Slow down and keep attention fresh.

3. Sort and mark the strips.
   - Batch strips into groups of 13 per flag.
   - Mark the back of each strip with the flag number.
   - Mark each strip position and intended color on the back so the strips can be stained separately and reassembled correctly.

4. Clamp for shaping.
   - Clamp the 13 loose strips together for one flag.
   - Do not glue yet.
   - The source prefers shaping before staining and gluing so the strips can still be separated for cleaner color work.

5. Grind the wave.
   - Use a grinder to carve a wavy surface across the clamped strip set.
   - Avoid flat spots.
   - Let the wave pattern be natural; the exact contour is not described as a strict template.
   - Source estimate: grinding and sanding take about 20 to 30 minutes per flag.

6. Sand the carved surface.
   - Use 60 or 80 grit sandpaper to remove harsh grinder marks.
   - Leave some grinder character if desired.
   - Expect heavy dust and mess.

7. Burn the surface.
   - Use a propane torch to burn the wood surface.
   - The source says a small plumbing torch can work, but will be much slower.
   - Watch the torch hose and fittings carefully. The source mentions a hose-clamp mishap.

8. Separate for staining.
   - After burning, separate the strips again.
   - Keep the back-side identification marks visible and organized.
   - Group strips by color so red, white, and blue can be stained or painted cleanly.

9. Create clean color boundaries.
   - Use razor blades tapped in with a hammer to separate color areas where blue meets red or white.
   - This helps create a crisp line without relying on tape across the wavy surface.
   - The source mentions spacing out the union area with an acrylic guide, but does not give the exact guide dimensions.

10. Stain and paint the strips.
   - Red stripes: Varathane Barn Red.
   - Blue union: Varathane Worn Navy.
   - White stripes: diluted white paint.
   - Keep the strips separated while coloring so colors do not bleed into each other.

11. Glue the flag.
   - Reassemble the strips in their numbered order.
   - Apply glue only to the bottom edge of each strip to reduce squeeze-out on the visible top face.
   - Clamp the flag flat and aligned.
   - Clean any squeeze-out with water and drying, or wait until the glue becomes tacky and scrape carefully.
   - Do not leave glue residue before finishing.

12. Make the star stencils.
   - The source uses a Cricut machine.
   - The star stencil is designed to fit the actual union width after the strips are clamped.
   - At 1 in per strip, the union would theoretically be about 7 in wide, but the source notes that squeeze and assembly made it a little under 7 in.
   - The Cricut file can be resized to the actual measured width.
   - The source prints three stencils at a time on a 24 in mat.
   - Pull each star from the stencil before use.

13. Apply and spray the stars.
   - Stick the stencil onto the wavy union area.
   - Tape off all four corners around the stencil.
   - Use cardboard to shield the rest of the flag.
   - Spray white paint from about 10 in above, pointing straight down.
   - Keep the can vertical to reduce overspray and leakage.
   - After drying, touch up fuzzy star edges with a sharp tool if needed.

14. Apply the first polyurethane coat.
   - Use fast-drying water-based polyurethane.
   - The source prefers the highest-gloss option available.
   - Let it dry according to product instructions; the source says it dries in about 30 minutes between coats.
   - Use crumpled brown paper to smooth between coats while keeping the finish shiny and smooth.

15. Shape the back ripple.
   - After the first polyurethane coat, grind the back edge.
   - Match the back contour to the top wave.
   - The source aims for about 1 in thickness at the back wave, but says it can be thinner.
   - This back ripple helps the flag look wavy when hanging on the wall and adds visual interest.

16. Apply final polyurethane.
   - Apply the final coat after the back contour is shaped.
   - The source uses two coats total and says two or three coats are enough.

17. Add hanging hardware.
   - Flip the finished flag over.
   - Add D-rings to the back for wall hanging.
   - Confirm the hardware is positioned so the flag hangs level.

## Safety Notes

- Ripping 130 narrow strips is repetitive and risky. Use safe table-saw setup, appropriate push tools, and breaks.
- Grinding and sanding are dusty and tiring. Use mask or respirator, hearing protection, and eye protection.
- Burning wood adds fire and fume risk. Keep a clear area, check torch fittings, and keep fire control nearby.
- Spray paint and polyurethane need ventilation.
- Razor blades used as color barriers are sharp and should be handled deliberately.

## Batch / Production Notes

- This method is efficient only if batching is organized well.
- Marking every strip prevents reassembly confusion after staining.
- Staining strips before glue-up avoids taping every stripe on a wavy assembled surface.
- Glue squeeze-out is a quality risk because it can interfere with the visible finish.
- The star stencil process is a bottleneck. Pulling star cutouts from the Cricut stencil takes time.
- Practicing on one flag before batching is recommended. The source says making one may not take too long, but ten is a long process.
