## Finished Configuration

- 13 horizontal stripes, each presenting the 1 1/2 in. edge of a 2×4.
- Seven 37 in. 2×4 blanks are split lengthwise along offset wave profiles.
- This produces 14 rough strips; use numbered strips 1–13 and retain the extra as scrap or a spare.
- Approximate flag proportions are 19 1/2 in. high × 37 in. wide, matching the American flag's 10:19 ratio.
- The standard build uses five waves across the 37 in. length.

## Tools

### Core Tools

- Jigsaw with a suitable wood-cutting blade
- 4 1/2 in. angle grinder with an 80-grit flap/sanding disc
- Clamps capable of holding all 13 stripes together
- Tape measure, pencil, and square
- Sanding block or sandpaper for burr removal

### Optional Upgrade Tools

- Random-orbit sander with 60-grit paper for smoother, rounder wave crests
- Wood-carving disc; the creator prefers the sanding disc
- Propane torch for lightly burning and highlighting the grain
- Dremel/rotary tool with an engraving cutter for carved natural-wood stars
- Keyhole router bit for concealed hanging slots

### Safety Equipment

- Safety glasses or face shield
- Hearing protection
- Properly fitted dust mask or respirator
- Outdoor work area or strong dust extraction/ventilation
- Fire extinguisher when burning the wood

## Build Instructions

### 1. Prepare the Flag Blanks

1. Select straight, dry 2×4 stock with minimal twist, cup, or large knots near the wave cuts.
2. Crosscut seven blanks to exactly 37 in.
3. Keep all blanks oriented consistently so the grain and best faces can be selected during assembly.

### 2. Make the Reusable Wave Jig

1. Cut the jig blank to 48 in. long and at least 2 in. tall. Thickness may be 1/4–3/4 in.
2. Download and print the official wave profile at the plan's specified scale.
3. Place the profile at one end of the jig blank and transfer it repeatedly until the five-wave line runs the full length.
4. Cut carefully along the transferred line with the jigsaw.
5. At the jig's end, mark 13 indexing stations at 1/2 in. spacing.
6. Extend each mark square across the indexing area and label the stations 1 through 13.
7. Sand only enough to remove roughness. Avoid changing the wave profile.

> The 1/2 in. station spacing creates approximately 6 1/2 in. of total progression across the 13 stripes. Wider spacing lays the wave over more dramatically; changing it also changes the final appearance.

### 3. Trace and Cut the 13 Stripes

1. Stand the first 37 in. blank and the wave jig on edge with both resting flat on the workbench.
2. Align the blank with jig station 1 and trace the complete wave profile.
3. Mark the resulting strip **1** on its bottom or back edge.
4. Cut along the wave line with the jigsaw.
5. Return the remaining portion of that blank to the jig, align it with station 2, and trace the next strip.
6. Label that strip **2**, then cut it out.
7. Repeat this process across the remaining blanks, advancing through stations 3–13.
8. The seventh blank produces strip 13 plus one unused strip.
9. Lightly sand away jigsaw burrs and tear-out without rounding over the glue edges.

### 4. Dry-assemble and Secure the Flag

1. Arrange strips 1–13 in numerical order and with consistent 37 in. ends.
2. Verify that the wave progresses smoothly and that no strip is reversed.
3. Clamp one half of the assembly while carving the exposed half, then reposition the clamps to carve the other half.
4. Place straight scrap cauls between the clamps and flag to hold every strip flat.
5. If ordinary bar clamps bow or lift the work, use a doubled caul or 2×6 caul so the clamp bears firmly against it. Parallel clamps are easier to keep flat.

### 5. Carve the Fabric-like Contour

1. With an 80-grit grinder disc, knock down the highest ridges left by the jigsaw cuts.
2. Make long, straight, controlled passes that follow the wave direction.
3. Blend neighboring strips together so the surface reads as one continuous piece of fabric.
4. Work from both sides as needed for comfortable grinder control.
5. Reduce the grinder angle if a worn disc begins scorching the wood.
6. Reposition the clamps and cauls, then complete the previously covered area.
7. Optionally use a random-orbit sander with 60-grit paper to round and blend the highest points.
8. Optionally remove additional material from outside strips 1 and 13 to deepen the edge-wave effect.
9. Stop when the transitions are visually smooth. Minor blemishes may be concealed by the optional burn and wash-style paint.

> [!CAUTION]
> Grinding creates a large volume of fine airborne dust. Perform this operation outdoors or with effective extraction, ventilation, eye protection, and respiratory protection.

### 6. Optional Grain Burn

1. Remove all loose dust and move to a fire-safe, ventilated area.
2. Pass a torch lightly and continuously over the face to bring out the grain and soften minor carving blemishes.
3. Do not dwell in one spot or char glue surfaces.
4. Allow the work to cool fully, then brush or wipe away loose soot.

### 7. Lay Out the Canton and Stars

1. Reassemble strips 1–13 in order and clamp them flat with scrap cauls.
2. Locate the canton so it spans the upper seven stripes, leaving six full stripes below it.
3. Square the stencil to the flag's top and hoist-side edge.
4. Draw the canton's straight vertical boundary. Keep this line straight; do not make it follow the carved wave.
5. If the star stencil is too short for the carved surface, establish reference ticks approximately 1/8–3/16 in. above an easy-to-see flat reference area.
6. Trace one star row, shift the semi-transparent stencil to the next reference tick, realign it with the canton boundary, and continue until all 50 stars are positioned.
7. Painter's tape may be used as a temporary surface for the reference ticks.

> Exact canton dimensions are not stated in the video. Use the official plan/stencil placement rather than estimating them from the finished 37 × 19 1/2 in. size.

### 8. Paint the Stripes and Canton

1. Separate the numbered strips while preserving their order and orientation.
2. Choose the flag's final top-to-bottom orientation. Paint the top stripe red, then alternate colors so both the top and bottom stripes are red. The finished flag must have seven red stripes and six natural-wood/white stripes. If jig strip 1 is placed at the top, the odd-numbered strips are red; reverse that assignment if the strip order is reversed.
3. Leave the six alternating stripes natural, or paint them white if that finish is preferred.
4. Paint the canton blue up to the straight boundary marked earlier.
5. For a translucent, grain-revealing finish, wipe excess acrylic paint from the surface with a paper towel while wet.
6. Alternatively, thin the acrylic paint with approximately 20% water before application.
7. Allow all paint to dry before handling or gluing.

### 9. Choose a Star Method

#### Option A — White Adhesive Stars

1. Paint and dry the blue canton.
2. Use the stencil to position all 50 white adhesive stars.
3. Press each star firmly onto the wood.
4. Seal the completed flag with a compatible clear coat to prevent lifted edges.

#### Option B — Natural-Wood Masked Stars

1. Position all 50 adhesive stars on bare wood before painting the canton.
2. Press every edge down firmly.
3. Paint the canton blue and allow it to dry.
4. Lift each sticker with a pick or fingernail to expose a natural-wood star.

#### Option C — Engraved Natural-Wood Stars

1. Paint and dry the blue canton.
2. Trace all 50 stars through the stencil.
3. Use a Dremel engraving cutter to remove the blue surface color inside each star and reveal natural wood.

### 10. Glue the Flag

1. Dry-assemble strips 1–13 one final time and confirm the wave, stripe colors, and canton alignment.
2. Turn the strips onto their joining edges while maintaining order.
3. Apply a controlled bead of Titebond III to every joint. Bias the glue toward the back/bottom edge so any squeeze-out is less likely to reach the finished face.
4. Return all strips to position and loosely install the clamps.
5. Place straight cauls across the assembly to hold the face flat.
6. Tighten the clamps gradually and evenly. Confirm that strips have not lifted, rolled, or shifted out of alignment.
7. Remove accessible squeeze-out without smearing glue across the painted face.
8. Leave the assembly clamped for the adhesive manufacturer's specified time and allow full cure before hanging.

### 11. Finish and Hang

1. Touch up only where necessary; resanding a painted error may also require reburning and repainting the full affected strip.
2. Apply a compatible clear coat, especially when adhesive stars are used.
3. Install picture-frame hardware for horizontal or vertical display, or rout keyhole slots into the back for concealed hanging.
4. Use anchors and fasteners rated for the completed flag's actual weight and wall construction.
5. For outdoor display, use exterior-rated glue, coating, and mounting hardware.
